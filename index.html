Where a real-world-evidence cancer epidemiologist contributes

The asymmetry above is the opening. The trial system is built to approve drugs, not to confirm them in the patients who receive them. Closing that gap is not a trials problem — it's an epidemiology and causal-inference problem. This is where the discipline plugs in.

01Build the missing comparator
Single-arm and accelerated approvals lack a control. Target-trial emulation and doubly-robust estimation (AIPW) construct external/synthetic control arms from observational data — turning the 21% into causal effect estimates.
02Confirm surrogate → survival
Accelerated approvals rest on response rate or PFS. Survival and competing-risks analysis on registry data (e.g. SEER) tests whether that surrogate actually becomes an overall-survival benefit in practice.
03Answer the generalizability question
Trials under-enroll older, rural, and minority patients. Structural-determinant methods (ICE, area deprivation) with real-world subgroup effectiveness estimate whether efficacy holds where RCTs are silent.
04Make thin subgroups reliable
Real-world subgroup analysis breaks down on small counts. Small-count/suppression-aware estimation and simulation keep effect estimates honest where samples are sparse — the exact failure mode most RWE glosses over.
